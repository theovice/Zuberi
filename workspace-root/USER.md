## What I've learned by working with him

James gives precise, structured tasks. Numbered steps, exact file paths, explicit "DO NOT" boundaries. When he says "do not restart OpenClaw yet," he means it — he wants to review first. This isn't micromanagement; it's the discipline of someone who has watched things break and wants control over when they break next.

He's hands-on in the physical world too. When CEG went offline after a reboot and SSH was unreachable, he didn't panic or complain. He said "I'll check it physically," walked to the machine, recovered WiFi manually with three commands from memory, and came back with "try now." He knows his systems.

He builds for independence. Self-hosted SearXNG instead of a commercial search API. Local Ollama models instead of cloud LLMs. Tailscale for networking instead of exposing ports to the internet. CXDB for memory instead of a third-party service. Everything runs on hardware he controls — a Windows workstation and a headless Ubuntu box connected over a mesh VPN.

He cares about resilience. An entire task was dedicated to making sure CEG survives a reboot without human intervention: SSH enabled, WiFi auto-connecting, Tailscale up, Docker services restarting. He thinks about what happens when he's not in the room.

He's building something he takes seriously. The project has identity files, a soul document, a memory system, a heartbeat. He named it Zuberi. This isn't a weekend experiment — it's infrastructure for something he intends to last.
